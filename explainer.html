Erdős problem 617 · the case r = 5

Twenty-five points, five colours

Can 26 points be joined by edges of five colours so that every six of them show all five? Erdős and Gyárfás conjectured no in 1999, and proved the analogous statements for three and four colours; five was the first open case. This page explains the result of a machine-checked candidate resolution — the answer is no, and 25 points is exactly the limit — along with the construction that achieves 25, the shape of the impossibility proof, and how the proof was produced and checked.

The question

Take n points and draw all n(n−1)/2 edges between them — the complete graph Kn. Colour every edge with one of r colours. Call the colouring balanced if every set of r+1 points contains edges of all r colours. Balance gets harder as n grows: more six-point sets to satisfy, and any subset of a balanced colouring is balanced, so once it fails at some n it fails forever after. Write N(r) for the largest n where a balanced r-colouring of Kn exists.

Two colours already tell an interesting story, and it fits on five points.

So for two colours, 2² + 1 = 5 points can be balanced and six cannot. Erdős and Gyárfás observed that two is the exception: for every r ≥ 3, they conjectured, balance already fails at r² + 1, so N(r) = r². They proved it for r = 3 and r = 4. The two halves of the r = 5 line are different kinds of object. N(5) ≥ 25 needs one good colouring of K25, and there is a clean one to show. N(5) ≤ 25 needs a proof that none of the roughly 5325 colourings of K26 is balanced — that is Erdős problem 617, and it is where the machinery comes in.

Twenty-five points: colour by direction

Arrange 25 points as a 5×5 grid and do arithmetic modulo 5, so the grid is the affine plane over the field of five elements. A line is a set of five points of constant slope; the possible slopes are 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 and vertical — six directions in all, and any two points lie on exactly one line. Lines of nonzero slope can wrap around the edges of the grid, torus-style.

Colour every edge by the direction of the line through its two endpoints. That gives six colour classes; to get five, merge two directions — here slopes 0 and 1 — into a single colour. This is the colouring below, and it is balanced.

Why it works is one pigeonhole. Fix any direction: its five parallel lines partition the 25 points, so among any six points, two must share a line of that direction — which is precisely an edge of that direction's colour. Every direction therefore shows up inside every six-point set, and merging two directions into one colour keeps all five colours present. The same construction produces a balanced colouring of Kr² whenever r is a prime power, which is the known reason the conjecture's "+1" cannot be dropped.

One vertex more, though, and the geometry has no room: a 26th point sits on no line of the plane. This project proved (as a warm-up, by hand, for every prime power r ≥ 3) that merged direction colourings can never be extended by any one additional point — for r = 5, a 26th — and then the real question is whether some entirely different colouring of K26 works instead.

How it was found

The project ran as a loop between search and proof. Searching for balanced colourings of K25 is easy — a local-search program finds one from a random start in seconds, and found dozens of structurally different ones. The same search on K26 always stalls: every run gets stuck around 800 unsatisfied six-point sets, and in the stuck states the violations all pass through a single vertex pair. The wall was suspiciously consistent, and its shape suggested what to prove.

Every conjectured lemma went through the same discipline: test it computationally on small cases first, prove it only if it survives. Several plausible lemmas died this way — a solver refuted one of them with an explicit 80-edge counterexample — and one early misreading of the literature was caught the same way. The two lemmas that survived were proved, then attacked: fresh sessions with no stake in the proofs reviewed them adversarially, found two genuine gaps, and both were repaired. The final step was formalization: every definition, both lemmas, the chain, and the classical ingredients were rebuilt in the Lean proof assistant, whose small kernel re-checks every inference. The four finite facts enter as solver certificates re-verified by a formally proven checker; the 1981 Brouwer bound and a 2005 classification of the extremal graphs (Kang–Pikhurko) were formalized from scratch rather than assumed — the classification by descending through nested cones, 21 → 17 → 13 → 9 points, to a nine-point base case with exactly two solutions.

What exactly has been established

The end state is a single machine-checked theorem: no balanced 5-colouring of K26 exists — stated in Lean over an arbitrary 26-element vertex set, checked by the Lean kernel, with no remaining mathematical hypothesis. Trust reduces to the proof assistant's kernel, three standard axioms, and fourteen named reflection axioms covering the SAT certificates and a few concrete finite-graph computations, all disclosed and audited. The result has not yet been reviewed by an independent human mathematician, and until it is, it should be described as a machine-verified candidate: the honest strength of the claim is "a proof object exists and checks", not "the community has accepted it".
