Erdős problem 617 · the case r = 5

Twenty-five points, five colours

Can 26 points be joined by edges of five colours so that every six of them show all five? Erdős and Gyárfás conjectured no in 1999, and proved the analogous statements for three and four colours; five was the first open case. This page explains the result of a machine-checked candidate resolution — the answer is no, and 25 points is exactly the limit — along with the construction that achieves 25, two independent proofs of the impossibility that were found by two separately-run AI systems, what happened when each was set loose on the other's work, and exactly how much of all this a careful reader still has to take on trust.

The question

Take n points and draw all n(n−1)/2 edges between them — the complete graph Kn. Colour every edge with one of r colours. Call the colouring balanced if every set of r+1 points contains edges of all r colours. Balance gets harder as n grows: more six-point sets to satisfy, and any subset of a balanced colouring is balanced, so once it fails at some n it fails forever after. Write N(r) for the largest n where a balanced r-colouring of Kn exists.

Two colours already tell an interesting story, and it fits on five points.

So for two colours, 2² + 1 = 5 points can be balanced and six cannot. Erdős and Gyárfás observed that two is the exception: for every r ≥ 3, they conjectured, balance already fails at r² + 1, so N(r) = r². They proved it for r = 3 and r = 4. The two halves of the r = 5 line are different kinds of object. N(5) ≥ 25 needs one good colouring of K25, and there is a clean one to show. N(5) ≤ 25 needs a proof that none of the roughly 5325 colourings of K26 is balanced — that is Erdős problem 617, and it is where the machinery comes in.

Twenty-five points: colour by direction

Arrange 25 points as a 5×5 grid and do arithmetic modulo 5, so the grid is the affine plane over the field of five elements. A line is a set of five points of constant slope; the possible slopes are 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 and vertical — six directions in all, and any two points lie on exactly one line. Lines of nonzero slope can wrap around the edges of the grid, torus-style.

Colour every edge by the direction of the line through its two endpoints. That gives six colour classes; to get five, merge two directions — here slopes 0 and 1 — into a single colour. This is the colouring below, and it is balanced.

Why it works is one pigeonhole. Fix any direction: its five parallel lines partition the 25 points, so among any six points, two must share a line of that direction — which is precisely an edge of that direction's colour. Every direction therefore shows up inside every six-point set, and merging two directions into one colour keeps all five colours present. The same construction produces a balanced colouring of Kr² whenever r is a prime power, which is the known reason the conjecture's "+1" cannot be dropped.

One vertex more, though, and the geometry has no room: a 26th point sits on no line of the plane. This project proved (as a warm-up, by hand, for every prime power r ≥ 3) that merged direction colourings can never be extended by any one additional point — for r = 5, a 26th — and then the real question is whether some entirely different colouring of K26 works instead.

Twenty-six points: why nothing works — proof one

There are now two independent proofs that no balanced colouring of K26 exists. They were produced by two AI systems run separately (more on that below), they share no intermediate steps, and each has been machine-checked and adversarially reviewed by the other side. The first — the one this project found — runs by contradiction, and its skeleton fits in one diagram. Suppose some balanced 5-colouring of K26 exists.

The two lemmas are where the difficulty actually lives; the chain around them is a page of careful bookkeeping. Their proofs are ordinary combinatorics — counting, extremal graph theory, case analysis — but the cases are numerous and unforgiving, which is why every step was ultimately re-done inside a proof assistant.

In one sentence: delete a vertex, and balance forces the survivors to hand you five perfect five-point blocking sets — then the poorest colour can't afford its own blocking set, and the whole structure collapses. The argument is local: it studies the neighbourhood structure around one deleted vertex.

The same theorem, a second way

The second proof never deletes a vertex. It goes straight at the poorest colour and squeezes it globally. Its central quantity: for a set Q of five mutually unjoined points in the poorest colour's graph, let P(Q) be the total number of that colour's edges leaving Q — the "boundary weight" of Q.

The two proofs use different classical ingredients, too. Proof one leans on Brouwer's 1981 bound plus the Kang–Pikhurko equality classification; proof two replaces the classification with Bollobás–Nikiforov's degree-sum theorem and never needs it. Where proof one's hardest human steps were found by watching search experiments wall (the phase-transition data of Figure 5), proof two was engineered directly: its author built an industrial certificate pipeline — 65 load-bearing SAT certificates with dual independent checkers and deliberate perturbation controls (loosen any constraint by one unit and the formulas become satisfiable, proving the encodings aren't vacuous).

Two systems, one theorem: the experiment

The two proofs exist because the project's owner ran a deliberate control experiment. After the first proof was complete and internally reviewed, a second AI system (OpenAI's gpt-5.6-sol, running alone) was given the same starting scaffold — the same problem statement, the same referee script, and the truthful information that the theorem had already been proved by another system — and asked to confirm it independently, without reading the first team's repository and without being told the route.

The comparison must be read honestly: the second run knew the answer was "no", knew a proof existed, and started from a polished scaffold after tractability was established. The first run was a cold open on a problem of unknown status that had to invent its own targets. So the timing figures below are characteristics of two different regimes — discovery versus confirmation — not a race result. One more wrinkle worth stating precisely: the same base model (gpt-5.6-sol) appears on both sides, in different roles — inside the first team it proved the two core lemmas from briefs written for it; in the control it worked alone end to end. The two systems — orchestration, methodology, verification pipelines — were entirely separate.

One detail from the independence audit deserves its own sentence. The control's only brush with the first team's work was accidental: a routine process listing showed a build command containing one of the first team's file names — 117 seconds after the control had already committed and reviewed its own version of the same idea (both teams had independently converged on Brouwer's 1981 theorem from the literature). The control system noticed the exposure itself, disclosed it in its notes with exact timestamps, quarantined the string, and banned process listings for the rest of its run — and every claim in that self-audit checks out against the session logs to the minute.

How the first proof was found

The project ran as a loop between search and proof. Searching for balanced colourings of K25 is easy — a local-search program finds one from a random start in seconds, and found dozens of structurally different ones. The same search on K26 always stalls: every run gets stuck around 800 unsatisfied six-point sets, and in the stuck states the violations all pass through a single vertex pair. The wall was suspiciously consistent, and its shape suggested what to prove.

The blocking-set structure of Lemma 1 gave a sharper probe. Colourings that contain a four-point blocking set — the thing Lemma 1 says cannot happen at 25 points — do exist on fewer points, and watching where they stop existing located the cliff exactly:

Every conjectured lemma went through the same discipline: test it computationally on small cases first, prove it only if it survives. Several plausible lemmas died this way — a solver refuted one of them with an explicit 80-edge counterexample — and one early misreading of the literature was caught the same way. The two lemmas that survived were proved, then attacked: fresh sessions with no stake in the proofs reviewed them adversarially, found two genuine gaps, and both were repaired. The final step was formalization: every definition, both lemmas, the chain, and the classical ingredients were rebuilt in the Lean proof assistant, whose small kernel re-checks every inference. The four finite facts enter as solver certificates re-verified by a formally proven checker; the 1981 Brouwer bound and a 2005 classification of the extremal graphs (Kang–Pikhurko) were formalized from scratch rather than assumed — the classification by descending through nested cones, 21 → 17 → 13 → 9 points, to a nine-point base case with exactly two solutions.

What exactly has been established

The end state is one theorem with two independent machine-checked proofs: no balanced 5-colouring of K26 exists — stated in Lean over an arbitrary 26-element vertex set, with a kernel-checked equivalence showing both formalizations prove the same statement, and with no remaining mathematical hypothesis on either side. The first proof's trust base is the proof assistant's kernel, three standard axioms, and four named reflection axioms covering the SAT certificates (the finite-graph computations that formerly also used reflection are now checked by the kernel directly). The second proof's trust base is exactly the three standard axioms — its certificates pass through Lean's kernel-checked LRAT path. Neither proof has been reviewed by an independent human mathematician, and until one is, this should be described as a machine-verified candidate: the honest strength of the claim is "two independent proof objects exist, check, and survived each other's hostile review", not "the community has accepted it".
